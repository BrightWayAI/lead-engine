# The 7 Buying Signals

The canonical taxonomy for every signal this plugin tracks. Use this to:
- **Classify** a captured signal (which of the 7 is it?)
- **Score** its strength (some signals are stronger than others)
- **Pick a drafting angle** (each signal has a different hook pattern that works)

If a signal genuinely doesn't fit any of these *and* doesn't match the user's custom signal in `user-context.md`, log it as `custom` with a free-form description rather than forcing it into a bucket.

---

## 1. Engagement

**Definition:** the contact engaged (liked, commented, shared, reacted) with content in your problem space within the last 14 days. Stronger if they *commented* with substance vs. just liked.

**Strength tiers:**
- **Strong:** posted a substantive comment with their own perspective, or shared the post with their own commentary.
- **Medium:** commented something short ("this!", "agree", "great point").
- **Weak:** just a like/reaction. Many people like-and-forget — tread carefully.

**ICP-fit check:** does the post they engaged with relate directly to a problem you solve, or is it tangential? Direct relation → keep. Tangential → low priority.

**Drafting angle:**
- Reference the *specific post* and *what they said*. "Saw your comment about [specific thing they said] on Jane's post" beats "saw your engagement."
- Resonance sentence should respond to *their* take, not the original post. Show you read what *they* wrote.
- Bridge to your offer through the substance of their comment.

**Common failure mode:** referencing the original post instead of their comment. Always reference *their* contribution, not the post they engaged with.

## 2. Job change

**Definition:** the contact started a new role at an ICP-fit company in the last 60 days. Best window is 14–45 days post-start (long enough they've taken stock, short enough they're still building their plan).

**Strength tiers:**
- **Strong:** lateral or upward move into a senior buyer-level role at a clearly ICP company. New office and new mandate.
- **Medium:** internal promotion, or new role at an ICP-adjacent company.
- **Weak:** more than 90 days in. By then they're set up.

**Drafting angle:**
- *Don't* lead with "congrats on the new role!" That's the cold-LinkedIn-spammer opener. If you want to acknowledge it, embed it in sentence two.
- Lead with a specific observation about *what they'll be working on* in the new role. Read their LinkedIn for the role description, the company's about page, recent funding, recent hires, etc.
- The bridge: tie your offer to a *first-90-days priority* they likely have. New VP RevOps? They're auditing the stack. New VP Marketing? They're cutting agency spend or planning a brand refresh.

**Common failure mode:** "Saw you joined [Co] — would love to chat about [generic offer]." This reads like every other DM in their inbox during their first month. Be specific or skip.

## 3. Funding

**Definition:** the contact's company raised in the last 90 days. Strongest in days 14–60 — past the announcement noise, into "we have money and need to spend it."

**Strength tiers:**
- **Strong:** Series A or B (still nimble, willing to try new vendors), led by a notable investor, with stated use of funds that touches your space.
- **Medium:** Series C/D, or seed with a clear product-market fit signal.
- **Weak:** post-IPO secondary, debt round, or undisclosed terms ("strategic investment").

**Drafting angle:**
- Reference the round specifically — amount, lead investor, *what they said the money is for*. Most funding announcements include a use-of-funds quote — find it and use it.
- The bridge: if their stated use overlaps your offer ("expanding sales team" + you sell sales infra → strong tie), lean into that. If not, find a second-order effect ("scaling sales means you'll need [X]").
- Avoid the cliché: "Saw your raise, congrats! Many of my clients use [vendor] post-funding." Cringe.

**Common failure mode:** spraying every recent fundraiser with the same template. The use-of-funds detail is what makes this signal strong; if you skip it, you're back to spray-and-pray.

## 4. Hiring

**Definition:** the company is posting roles that imply your problem space. The role they're hiring is a *proxy* for the work they need done.

**Strength tiers:**
- **Strong:** posting a senior role (Director+) explicitly tied to your problem (e.g., "Head of CRO" if you sell CRO services). They're spending serious budget to hire someone — they'd often rather buy a service in the meantime.
- **Medium:** posting an IC-level role in your space. Useful, less urgent.
- **Weak:** posting unrelated roles. Skip.

**Drafting angle:**
- Reference the *specific role* and *what the JD emphasizes*. "Saw the Head of CRO posting — looks like you're prioritizing checkout flow specifically based on the JD."
- The bridge: you can either be the *interim* solution while they hire ("this often takes 4–6 months — we can ship a teardown in 2 weeks while you're searching") or the *force multiplier* once they hire ("the new lead's first 90 days usually need outside help to set baseline").
- Don't pitch yourself as a replacement for the hire. Buyers don't react well to "you don't need to hire — just use us."

**Common failure mode:** treating the hiring company as the buyer when the actual buyer is the hiring manager. Make sure the contact you're messaging is the person hiring (their boss or peer), not someone unrelated at the same company.

## 5. Growth / expansion

**Definition:** company announcement of a new office, market entry, product line, or major partnership in the last 90 days.

**Strength tiers:**
- **Strong:** new market or new product that creates an obvious need for your offer (e.g., expanding to EU + you sell international compliance).
- **Medium:** new office or org-chart announcement that *implies* growing pain.
- **Weak:** vague "exciting new chapter" PR fluff with no specifics.

**Drafting angle:**
- Reference the specific expansion. "Saw the SF office announcement — usually means a hiring sprint and a comp-band realignment, both of which are when [your offer] gets useful."
- The bridge: scaling pain. Whatever you sell, frame your offer through "this gets harder when you 2x the surface area."

**Common failure mode:** treating expansion as a personal milestone for the contact. It's a *company* signal, not a *career* signal — the angle should be "what's getting harder" not "congrats!"

## 6. Tech-stack change

**Definition:** the company is adopting or dropping a tool in your category. Strongest when it's a competitor or adjacent tool to what you sell.

**Strength tiers:**
- **Strong:** they just churned off your direct competitor (publicly visible via job posts mentioning the old tool, BuiltWith data, case studies, etc.). They are *actively shopping*.
- **Medium:** they're adopting a tool adjacent to yours — common gotchas you can preempt.
- **Weak:** stack changes more than 6 months old.

**Drafting angle:**
- Lead with the specific change. "Saw [Company] just moved off [old tool] — usually means [common pain point]."
- The bridge: be the helper, not the seller. "Common gotcha when teams switch is [X] — happy to share how we handle it. No pitch."
- This is the highest-converting signal type for many B2B categories. Don't waste it with a generic pitch.

**Common failure mode:** mentioning the stack change but not the specific gotcha. The whole reason this signal works is that you have *specific knowledge* about that tool transition.

---

## 7. Direct intent

**Definition:** they explicitly asked for what you sell. Posted a question. Asked for recommendations. Described a problem you solve.

**Strength tiers:**
- **Strong:** posted in the last 7 days asking for vendor recs, advice, or describing a problem you solve specifically.
- **Medium:** posted a related question without explicit recs request.
- **Weak:** complaint-tweet without a clear "looking for help" framing — could just be venting.

**Drafting angle:**
- *Answer the question first.* Lead with substance, not a pitch.
- Sentence 1: name the thread / question. Sentence 2-3: actually answer it (briefly, but well). Sentence 4: very soft pitch — "we run into this a lot if you want to swap notes" — only if the answer earned it.
- This is the highest-conversion signal *and* the easiest to screw up. Pitching too early reads predatory.

**Common failure mode:** treating the question as a sales opportunity rather than a chance to be useful. Be useful first. Even if they don't book a call, you've earned visibility — they'll remember you next time.

## Custom signals

The user may have configured a custom signal in `user-context.md` (e.g., "earnings miss in retail vertical", "specific certification announcement"). When you see a signal that fits the custom pattern, classify it as `custom: [their description]` and apply general principles:

- Reference the specific event.
- Show you understand *why it matters* in their world.
- Bridge to your offer through second-order effects.

## The scoring rubric (used by `/lead-capture` and `/lead-pull`)

Combine three factors:

**ICP fit:** does the contact's role + company match the user's ICP (from `user-context.md`)?
- Match: +2
- Adjacent: +1
- No match: drop the signal

**Signal strength** (per the tiers above):
- Strong: +2
- Medium: +1
- Weak: 0

**Recency:**
- Last 7 days: +2
- Last 30 days: +1
- Older: 0

**Disqualifier:** if any from `user-context.md` triggers, drop regardless of score.

**Total → priority:**
- 5–6: High (queue for today)
- 3–4: Medium (queue for this week)
- 0–2: Low (log but don't queue)

This is a guideline, not a rule. If a Medium signal is *exactly* in the user's strike zone, promote it to High and trust the gut.
